Project Team 9: Topic, COSI 116A F23

Xiaoran Liu, Zixin Zhang

Project-long Course Project as part of COSI 116A: Information Visualization, taught by Prof. Dylan Cashman, Brandeis University.

Motivation

The project is driven by an interest in exploring the dynamics of educational spending per student and its impact on educational outcomes across different states in the United States. We seek to understand if there's a direct correlation between expenditure and student performance and how this relationship has evolved over time. The aim is to provide insights that could guide policy decisions and resource allocation in education.

Visualization

  1. Color-Coded United States Maps: These maps are used to depict the trends in scores and expenditures over time. Each state is color-coded according to either its average student performance or educational spending, providing a geographical context to the data.

  2. Scatter Plot: This scatter plot is used to show the relationship between educational spending and NAEP test average score for each state. Each state is represented by a circle, with the x-axis representing the average expenditure per student and the y-axis representing the average score. The size of the circle is proportional to the number of students in the state. The scatter plot is interactive, allowing users to hover over each circle to see the state name on the plot.

  3. Heatmap: The heatmap illustrates the correlation between different types of educational expenditures and studentss' average NAEP test score, offering a quick and intuitive way to identify strong or weak correlations.

Visualization explanation

Data Analysis

Our data analysis involved two primary datasets: 'states.csv' and '2022.csv'. The 'states_all.csv' dataset encompassed a comprehensive range of variables including yearly educational expenditures, student demographics, and NAEP average score across various states. The '2022.csv' dataset provided a more recent snapshot of educational spending and the NAEP average score.

Both datasets included numerical data (e.g., expenditure amounts, student performance scores) and categorical data (e.g., state names). During preprocessing, we normalized expenditure figures to account for inflation and per-student spending differences, filled missing values using appropriate imputation methods, and converted data types for consistency and ease of analysis.

These datasets were chosen for their relevance to our goal of understanding the relationship between educational spending and student outcomes at the state level. Their comprehensive nature allowed for a multifaceted analysis, ensuring our results would be robust and replicable.

Task Analysis

The primary tasks involved in our analysis were identifying trends in educational spending, correlating these trends with student performance metrics, and visualizing geographical variances. The goal was to provide actionable insights into how different spending strategies impact student outcomes and to highlight any regional disparities that might inform policy decisions.

Abstract tasks included data cleansing, normalization, trend analysis, correlation detection, and data visualization. These tasks were crucial in transforming raw data into meaningful insights.

Design Process

Our design process began with basic sketches of potential visualizations, including scatter plots, heatmaps, and geographical maps. Through iterative refinement, these evolved into more sophisticated visualizations that effectively communicated complex data relationships.

Design decisions were grounded in data visualization theory, focusing on clarity, accuracy, and accessibility. Feedback from initial user testing led to adjustments in color schemes for better visibility and enhancements in the interactivity of the visualizations to improve user engagement and understanding.

Conclusion

The project successfully highlighted key insights into the relationship between educational spending and student performance. It became evident that higher spending does not always correlate with improved outcomes, and regional disparities are significant. The visualizations provided a clear, engaging way to communicate these complex relationships.

For future work, integrating more granular data, such as district-level spending or socio-economic factors, could offer a deeper understanding. Longitudinal studies to track the impact of educational policies over time would also be valuable.

This project demonstrates the power of data analysis and visualization in uncovering and communicating trends in education, offering a foundation for more informed decision-making in this vital domain.
